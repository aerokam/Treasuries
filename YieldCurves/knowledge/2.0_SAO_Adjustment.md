# 2.0 SA with outlier adjustment (SAO)

## Overview
The standard seasonal adjustment (SA) transform (market price → SA price → SA Yield) removes predictable, recurring CPI seasonality. The resulting SA real-yield curve still has "wiggles" — points sitting above or below a smooth curve. The **SAO** step removes those wiggles by fitting the SA yields to a **smooth fair-value curve** and reading each TIPS off it.

## Inspired by Canty's outlier analysis, but operationally a curve smoother
Our SAO step is **inspired by Canty's outlier-factor analysis** ([Canty.md](Canty.md), Eq 20–21), in which the inflation index decomposes as `I_t = T_t · S_t · O_t` and the outlier index `O_t` captures **non-seasonal** effects on top of trend and seasonality. Canty determines `O_t` **analytically, per known event** — a VAT hike, a gasoline move since the last CPI print — i.e. specific, identifiable, non-seasonal inflation shocks.

We are **not able to identify specific outlier factors** that way. So instead of constructing an `O_t`, we **simply smooth the curve**, on the working assumption that a TIPS's deviation from a smooth curve is driven by factors **not particularly relevant to the retail buy-and-hold investor, as far as we know** (e.g. liquidity or other relative-value effects, to which such a holder is indifferent). In short: Canty isolates known outlier factors and adjusts for them; we cannot isolate them, so we smooth them away — but the *motivation* (strip non-seasonal noise that distorts comparison across the curve) is the same. **This is not a reimplementation of Canty's `O_t`; do not conflate the two.**

## Why snap everything to the curve
For a buy-and-hold retail holder, a point sitting off a smooth curve only matters if a **value-relevant** factor explains it. The candidate factors were tested empirically in [2.2 SAO Residual Analysis](2.2_SAO_Residual_Analysis.md) and found immaterial:
- **Coupon and index ratio** — the cleanest evidence is **same-maturity-date pairs**: two TIPS maturing on the same date share the same seasonal factor, so any yield gap is pure coupon + index ratio. Across such pairs the coupon differs ~3.5× and the index ratio spans e.g. 1.35 vs 1.58, yet the SA yields differ by **~1 bp at most** (typically). That is strong evidence neither materially affects yield.
- Remaining deviations are structured by **maturity month** (a deseasonalization residual) or are **idiosyncratic** (e.g. a liquidity-driven richness). Neither changes fair value to a buy-and-hold holder — a liquidity cheapness just makes a bond "a better deal," not a different fair value.

So **every deviation that isn't value-relevant is smoothed out**: `SAO_i = smoothCurve(maturity_i)` for all TIPS. (If a value-relevant factor were ever identified, that — and only that — would justify an off-curve SAO.)

## Caveat: we may be smoothing away real expected-return differences
This smoothing is an **assumption, not a certainty.** We do not actually know that an apparently-artificial high yield *is* artificial. It may genuinely deliver a higher return to whoever buys it. The parallel is instructive: the SA step is really an adjustment to **expected nominal return** — it levels out the portion of return that is *seasonally predictable* so bonds maturing in different months can be compared fairly. By the same logic, we **suspect there are other factors that also affect expected nominal return**, but in ways that are **not seasonally predictable** and that we cannot model. If a deviation reflects one of those, smoothing it down hides a genuinely better (or worse) deal rather than removing noise. We accept that risk because, for the target holder, we have no identified value-relevant factor to justify keeping any given point off the curve — but the uncertainty is real and is the main known limitation of this approach.

## The smooth curve: Nelson-Siegel-Svensson
The curve is **Nelson-Siegel-Svensson (NSS)** — the Fed / Gürkaynak-Sack-Wright standard for fitting real-yield term structures, which guarantees a smooth, economically-shaped curve:
\[ y(\tau) = \beta_0 + \beta_1\frac{1-e^{-\tau/\lambda_1}}{\tau/\lambda_1} + \beta_2\left(\frac{1-e^{-\tau/\lambda_1}}{\tau/\lambda_1}-e^{-\tau/\lambda_1}\right) + \beta_3\left(\frac{1-e^{-\tau/\lambda_2}}{\tau/\lambda_2}-e^{-\tau/\lambda_2}\right) \]

- **Fit**: the two decay params `λ1, λ2` are grid-searched; for each pair the four `β` are linear and solved by OLS; the lowest-SSR fit wins.
- **Near-maturity (< 0.5 yr)**: excluded from the **fit** (SA yield there is price-noise-dominated), but still read off the curve.
- **Output**: `SAO_i = y(maturity_i)`. The per-bond **deviation** `SA_i − SAO_i` is retained as a rich/cheap relative-value diagnostic (shown in the drill-down), not as part of fair value.

The constant lives in `app.js` (`SAO_NOISE_YRS`); the fit is `fitNSS()` / `calculateSAO()`.
