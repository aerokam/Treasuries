# 2.2 SAO Outlier Factor — Empirical Analysis & Rationale

**Status:** Analysis supporting the **O (outlier)** step of [2.0 SAO](2.0_SAO_Adjustment.md).
**Dependencies:** [1.0 Seasonal Adjustments](1.0_Seasonal_Adjustments.md), [2.0 SAO](2.0_SAO_Adjustment.md), [Canty](Canty.md).
**Data snapshot:** FedInvest mid prices, settlement **2026-06-18**, region **2027–2031** maturities (the front end, where seasonality is material and the SA curve is otherwise flat at ~1.82–1.88%).

---

## 1. Purpose

The SAO ("O" = outlier) step smooths residual wiggles that remain in the **SA** curve after the standard seasonal transform. Historically the smoothing was a black-box regression blend with hand-tuned weights. This document establishes, **from the actual data**, *what* the residual is, *what it is not*, and therefore *why* it is legitimate to smooth it. It is meant to be shared so a reader can audit the reasoning rather than trust the knob settings.

> **Honesty note on "cause."** What is **demonstrated** below is an *empirical pattern* plus the *elimination* of the obvious fundamental explanations. The exact generating mechanism is **inferred, not proven**. We distinguish the two explicitly.

---

## 2. The pattern: a maturity-MONTH residual

Detrend the SA curve against a smooth quadratic in years-to-maturity, then group the residuals by **maturity month**:

| Maturity month | n | Mean residual | Individual residuals (bp) | Origin tenor* |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **April** | 7 | **−3.9 bp** | −13.8, −3.0, −4.3, −1.2, −2.2, −1.8, −0.8 | 5-yr |
| July | 5 | +0.8 bp | 3.2, −0.7, −1.6, 0.9, 1.9 | 10-yr |
| January | 8 | +1.3 bp | (spread ±, ~0) | 10-yr |
| **October** | 4 | **+3.2 bp** | 6.1, 4.4, 1.4, 1.0 | 5-yr |

\*Approximate original auction tenor by maturity month (5-yr TIPS issued Apr/Oct; 10-yr issued Jan/Jul; 30-yr matures Feb).

**Key facts:**
- **Sign is 100% consistent across maturity years.** Every April bond prints below the smooth trend; every October bond above.
- **Peak-to-trough swing ≈ 7 bp** (Oct +3.2 vs Apr −3.9), blowing out to **−13.8 bp** at the 2027-04 front rung.
- **It amortizes with maturity** — large at 2027, sub-1 bp by 2031 — matching the published understanding that TIPS seasonal effects are a front-end phenomenon that gets diluted over a longer holding period (Gürkaynak–Sack–Wright; StreetStats).
- `corr(residual, maturity-month-mean) = 0.66`.

---

## 3. What it is NOT (fundamental factors eliminated)

The candidate "valid reasons for a departure from a smooth curve" were tested directly.

### 3.1 Coupon — eliminated
The April-low pattern holds across coupons of **0.125%, 1.25%, 1.625%, 2.125%, 3.625%, 3.875%**. If coupon drove it, low- and high-coupon Aprils would split; instead *all* sit below trend.

**The decisive evidence — same-maturity-date pairs (a controlled experiment).** Two TIPS maturing on the same date share the *same* seasonal factor, so any yield gap between them is **purely coupon + index ratio**. These pairs differ in coupon by up to ~3.5× *and* in index ratio by up to ~2× — yet their SA yields differ by **~1 bp at most** (typically), the 2027-01 pair being the lone ~4.5 bp exception:

| Date | low cpn (index ratio) → SA | high cpn (index ratio) → SA | ΔSA |
|---|---|---|---|
| 2027-01 | 0.375% (1.37) → 1.811 | 2.375% (1.65) → 1.856 | +4.5 bp |
| 2028-01 | 0.500% (1.35) → 1.870 | 1.750% (1.58) → 1.883 | +1.3 bp |
| 2028-04 | 1.250% (1.11) → 1.784 | 3.625% (2.05) → 1.798 | +1.4 bp |
| 2029-04 | 2.125% (1.07) → 1.815 | 3.875% (2.02) → 1.825 | +1.0 bp |

This is strong, near-controlled evidence that **neither coupon nor index ratio materially affects yield**: hold the maturity date fixed, vary coupon ~3.5× and index ratio ~2×, and the yield barely moves. What residual coupon effect exists points the *opposite* way to a duration story — higher coupon → **higher** SA yield by ~1 bp, whereas higher coupon → lower duration would push it *down* on this flat/slightly-positive curve — so the ~1 bp is a tiny genuine coupon premium, not a curve artifact. At ~1 bp it is immaterial for fair value and is smoothed with everything else.

### 3.2 Index ratio — eliminated (and the directional hypothesis is contradicted)
Hypothesis: low index ratio (less accreted inflation, more deflation-floor left, less phantom-income drag) is desirable → higher demand → higher price → lower yield.

| Correlation with SA residual | r |
|---|---|
| Index ratio | **0.05** ≈ 0 |
| Maturity month | **0.66** |

Three confirmations it is not the driver:
1. Within the April group, index ratio spans **1.02 → 2.05**, yet every April bond is below trend regardless of IR.
2. **The directional prediction fails on the cleanest cut:** October has the **lowest** mean index ratio (1.07 — recent issues) but the **highest, most positive** residual (+3.2 bp) — the opposite of "low IR → low yield."
3. Index ratio is essentially an **issue-age / seasoning proxy** (mean IR by month: Jan 1.42, Jul 1.30, Apr 1.35, Oct 1.07), which has no business driving a clean-price real-yield curve, and empirically doesn't.

### 3.3 Canty's exact formula (RAI term + semiannual two-month weighting) — eliminated
Our SA transform uses Canty **Eq 14** (approximation): `price × (S_settle / S_maturity)`, a *single* maturity-month factor. Canty's exact **Eq 13** adds a Real-Accrued-Interest term, and **Eq 17** replaces the single factor with a coupon-weighted blend of the **two semiannual coupon-month** factors (an April-maturity TIPS pays in April *and* October).

We implemented Eq 17 and recomputed. Result: it moves the **2027-04 outlier by 0.0 bp**; the largest correction anywhere is **+1.1 bp**, only on high-coupon bonds.

**Why it can't explain the residual:** the Eq 17 / Eq 13 corrections are driven by the **coupon stream** (other-month weight 0.1–5%), but the residual rides on the **principal at maturity**, which both formulas place in the maturity month identically. The residual is therefore **coupon-independent** — confirming it is a property of the **maturity-month seasonal factor itself**, not of the cashflow weighting.

### 3.4 Original-tenor / liquidity cohort — eliminated
Apr and Oct maturities are both **5-yr-origin** TIPS, yet they carry **opposite-signed** residuals (Apr −3.9, Oct +3.2). A tenor/liquidity cohort effect would push both the same way. It splits along the **seasonal** axis, not the tenor axis.

---

## 4. Inferred mechanism (not proven)

With coupon, index ratio, Canty's RAI/semiannual refinements, and tenor-cohort all eliminated, and the residual being **coupon-independent, maturity-month-keyed, sign-consistent across years, and amortizing toward the long end**, the most consistent remaining explanation is:

> **Imperfect deseasonalization.** Our daily SA factor series is constructed by 31 CFR App. B interpolation of monthly BLS CPI-SA — a *necessarily calculated* daily SA Ref CPI (there is no official one; see [1.0](1.0_Seasonal_Adjustments.md)). The institutional desks that actually price the curve use their own seasonal model. The mismatch between the two is, by construction, **keyed to the calendar month** and applied to the dominant principal cashflow — producing precisely a month-of-maturity residual that is largest where the seasonal correction itself is largest (the front end) and washes out as it amortizes over longer maturities.

This is consistent with Canty's own framing: his **outliers ($O_t$)** are *transient* shocks (gasoline since the last CPI print, VAT changes) — explicitly **not** persistent recurring month effects. So this residual is **not a Canty outlier**; it is a deseasonalization residual that the empirical "O" smoothing step mops up. The user's intuition — "it's puzzling that there would be consistent maturity-month factors *other than* the seasonal adjustment" — is correct, and the resolution is that it **is** the seasonal adjustment: specifically its calibration error, not a new factor.

We cannot fully exclude a small genuine month-specific supply/demand component, but nothing in the available data supports one, and it cannot be separated from the deseasonalization residual without the desks' proprietary seasonal factors.

---

## 5. Consequence for the O step

The findings above (§3) do the decisive work: **no value-relevant factor explains the departures.** Coupon and index ratio — the only factors that would change fair value to a buy-and-hold holder — are immaterial. Everything else (the month-keyed deseasonalization residual of §2/§4, and any idiosyncratic per-bond richness such as the twice-confirmed Jan-2028 hump) is **relative-value noise**: a liquidity-driven cheapness just makes a bond "a better deal," not a different fair value, to a holder indifferent to liquidity.

So the O step does **not** target seasonality specifically. It **smooths every non-value-relevant deviation to a fair-value curve**: `SAO_i = smoothCurve(maturity_i)` via **Nelson-Siegel-Svensson** (see [2.0](2.0_SAO_Adjustment.md)). The maturity-month structure documented here is then simply *what the deviations look like*, not the thing being targeted — NSS removes month-keyed and idiosyncratic wiggles alike.

> **Note — inspired by, but not, Canty's outlier factor.** Canty's `O_t` is an *analytical, event-driven* adjustment for known inflation shocks not yet in the CPI; it is not a curve smoother (see [2.0](2.0_SAO_Adjustment.md) and [Canty.md](Canty.md) Eq 20–21). Lacking a way to identify specific outlier factors, we smooth instead. Our SAO is inspired by that analysis but is operationally a curve fit.

### 5.1 Caveat — we may be smoothing away real expected-return differences
This is an **assumption, not a certainty.** We do not actually know that an apparently-artificial high yield *is* artificial — it may genuinely deliver a higher return to whoever buys it. The SA step itself is really an adjustment to **expected nominal return**: it strips the *seasonally predictable* part so different maturity months compare fairly. By the same logic, we **suspect other factors also affect expected nominal return** but in ways that are **not seasonally predictable** and that we cannot model. If a smoothed-away deviation is one of those, we are hiding a genuinely better/worse deal, not removing noise. We accept that because, for the target holder, no value-relevant factor has been identified to justify keeping a point off the curve — but the uncertainty is real and is this approach's main known limitation.

**Prior approaches, for the record:** the original regression-blend was far too weak (at 2027-04 it corrected only 3 bp of a 13.8 bp deviation). An interim duration-amortized month de-mean removed the *seasonal* bias but, by construction, preserved idiosyncratic deviations like Jan-2028 — which the buy-and-hold objective says should also be smoothed. NSS supersedes both: it fits one smooth fair-value curve and snaps all points to it.

## 6. Extension: does the residual persist at the long end?

**§2–§5 above analyzed only the 2027–2031 front-end region.** [2.0](2.0_SAO_Adjustment.md) had generalized "smooth every deviation" to the whole curve on that basis alone — worth checking directly rather than assuming it extrapolates, since §2 already flagged the residual "amortizes with maturity."

**Method:** fit the same full-curve NSS curve `calculateSAO()` uses, then measure `|SA − NSS|` grouped by maturity year across the entire available curve (2027–2056), settlement 2026-07-01.

**Result — deviation from the full-curve NSS fit, by maturity year:**

| Maturity year | mean\|dev\| | max\|dev\| |
|---|---|---|
| 2027 | 6.4 bp | 15.2 bp |
| 2028 | 3.5 bp | 5.4 bp |
| 2029 | 1.5 bp | 3.4 bp |
| 2030 | 0.8 bp | 1.4 bp |
| 2031 | 1.5 bp | 2.0 bp |
| 2032 | 1.5 bp | 2.9 bp (2032-04 only; 2032-07 is +1.4bp) |
| 2033–2036 | 1.7–1.9 bp | ≤2.2 bp, no sign pattern |

Cross-checked with a maturity-agnostic **local-trend residual** (±0.6yr moving average of SA, independent of the global NSS shape) over 2030–2036 — same story: |resid| ≤ ~2.4bp throughout, and by 2032-07 onward there is no consistent April-low/October-high sign pattern left, just noise.

**Reading:** the front-end decay from §2 (large at 2027, sub-1bp by 2031) is real and continues — by 2030 the deviation is already at noise level, and the **2032-04 point (-2.4 to -2.9bp) is the last one showing a real, sign-consistent departure**; **2032-07 onward is clean**. Beyond 2036 there's a data gap (2037–2039 gap years) and the visible large deviations at 2040–2041 are a full-curve NSS **fit** artifact (the single curve straining to span a sparse, lumpy long tail), not a deseasonalization residual — consistent with §3's finding that value-relevant/idiosyncratic structure, not seasonality, explains long-end departures.

**Consequence:** smoothing should not apply uniformly to the whole curve. §5's `SAO_i = smoothCurve(maturity_i)` is refined in [2.0](2.0_SAO_Adjustment.md#the-smoothing-fades-out-with-maturity) to fade the snap-to-curve weight from 1 to 0 over `SAO_FADE_START_YRS=5` → `SAO_FADE_END_YRS=6` years-to-maturity, so points beyond ~6yr report their raw SA yield instead of being pulled onto a curve that was never shown to need correcting there.
