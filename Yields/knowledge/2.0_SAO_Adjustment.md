# 2.0 SA with outlier adjustment (SAO)

## Overview
While the standard seasonal adjustment (SA) transform (market price -> SA price -> SA Yield) removes predictable monthly seasonality, the resulting "SA Yield" curve can still exhibit significant "wiggles" or outliers, particularly at the front end (short-dated maturities). These are often caused by short-term market idiosyncraticies or specific seasonal anomalies that the standard factors don't fully capture.

The **Outlier (O) Adjustment** applies an empirical smoothing algorithm to the SA curve, resulting in the SAO (Seasonal Adjustment with Outlier adjustment) curve, which is intended to generate yields that reflect institutional TIPS traders views of outlier affects on the nominal returns.

## Backwards-Anchored Trend Fitting
The algorithm processes the yield curve from **longest maturity to shortest** (Right-to-Left).

### 1. The Anchor (Long End)
For bonds with maturities > 7 years or the last 4 bonds in the series, the SA Yields are considered stable enough to be used directly. In this region:
\[ SAO = SA \]

### 2. The Sliding Window (Medium to Short End)
As the algorithm moves toward shorter maturities, it uses a sliding window of the next 4 longer bonds to establish a projected trend line (Linear Regression).

- **Projected Yield**: The intercept of the linear regression established by the longer bonds at the current bond's maturity date.
- **Blending**: The final SAO yield is a weighted blend of this Projected trend and the bond's actual SA Yield.

### 3. Blend Weights
The `trendWeight` (how much to trust the longer-term trend vs. the current bond's SA yield) varies by maturity to prioritize smoothing at the volatile front end:
- **Very Short (< 0.5 years)**: 90% Trend (Highly smoothed).
- **Short (0.5 to 2 years)**: 15% Trend (Reduced smoothing to reflect SA reliability).
- **Medium (2 to 5 years)**: 25% Trend.
- **Long (> 5 years)**: 20% Trend (Default for non-anchor region).

The curve fitting algorithm naturally handles the relationship between maturities (including potential inversions) through these weighted trends, particularly at the shortest maturities where the trend weight is highest.
